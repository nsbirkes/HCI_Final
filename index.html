eBay.com Usability Testing Report

INF 650 - User Interface Design & Usability

Website: https://www.ebay.com

Sections: 1. Website · 2. Heuristic Eval · 3. Personas · 4. Scenarios · 5. Test Plan · 6. Results

Website Selection

The website selected for this usability test is eBay.com. eBay is one of the world's largest online marketplaces, serving both buyers searching for products and sellers listing items through fixed-price or auction formats. Its range of features - search and filtering, auction bidding, product listing, and checkout - provides multiple areas where usability issues can emerge, making it a strong candidate for evaluation.

Heuristic Evaluation

This evaluation was conducted as an individual expert review using Nielsen's 10 Usability Heuristics. Severity ratings use the standard 0-4 scale (0 = not a problem, 4 = catastrophic).

Personas

Two personas were developed to represent eBay's primary user segments. These are grounded in general e-commerce user research and informal conversations with representative users.

Persona 1: Sandra Mitchell - The Casual Seller

  • Age: 42
  • Job / Focus: Part-time retail associate and stay-at-home parent
  • Demographics: Married, suburban Midwest, moderate income, some college education
  • Goals: Sell unused household items to declutter and earn supplemental income with minimal time investment
  • Environment: Home laptop during school hours; occasional smartphone use
  • Technical Expertise: Moderate - comfortable with Amazon and Facebook Marketplace; has never listed anything on eBay
"I just want to list my stuff quickly and get paid - without all the confusion."

Persona 2: Marcus Chen - The Deal-Seeking Buyer

  • Age: 26
  • Job / Focus: IT Support Technician
  • Demographics: Single, urban apartment, bachelor's degree in IT, tech-enthusiast lifestyle
  • Goals: Find the best price on used electronics, monitor multiple auctions, and evaluate seller trust before committing
  • Environment: Home desktop as primary device; checks eBay on his smartphone throughout the workday
  • Technical Expertise: High - experienced eBay buyer, understands auction mechanics and how to read seller feedback
"I know exactly what I want - I just need to find the best deal before someone else does."

Scenarios

Scenario 1 - Sandra Mitchell | Goal: List a used item for sale

Sandra recently cleaned out her garage and found a box of her kids' old video games she no longer needs. Her neighbor mentioned making good money selling on eBay, so she sits down with her laptop during her lunch break to try it out. She has a basic eBay account from years ago but has never listed anything for sale. She is unsure about pricing, what photos to take, and whether to use an auction or fixed-price format. She wants the process to be intuitive enough to figure out on her own and hopes to have at least one item listed within the hour.

Scenario 2 - Marcus Chen | Goal: Evaluate and bid on a used graphics card

Marcus has a $200 budget for a used graphics card to upgrade his gaming PC and has been watching two eBay auctions for the past week. On his lunch break, he pulls up the auction ending in four hours - it is currently at $148 with three bidders. He wants to compare that listing against a Buy It Now option he saved at $189, check each seller's feedback score, and place a strategic maximum bid without driving up the price. He also wants to confirm his bid went through and make sure he will be notified if he is outbid.

Test Plan

5A. Goals for the Test (Whitney Quesenbery's 5Es)

  • Effective: Can users successfully complete core tasks - listing an item and finding and bidding on a product?
  • Efficient: How quickly and with how few errors do users accomplish those tasks?
  • Engaging: Do users find the interface appropriate and trustworthy for their intended purpose?
  • Error Tolerant: How well does the site prevent errors, and can users recover when errors occur?
  • Easy to Learn: Can a first-time seller navigate the listing process without prior instruction?

5B. Scope

  • Website: eBay.com (desktop browser)
  • Participants: 3 users
  • Session length: 45 minutes per participant

5C. Tasks

Tasks are written as measurable goals, not step-by-step directions.

Task 1 - List a Used Item
"You have a used video game you no longer play. Starting from the eBay homepage, please list it for sale."

Task 2 - Find and Bid on a Product
"You are looking for a used graphics card with a budget of $200. Find an auction listing, evaluate the seller, and place a bid."

Task 3 - Locate Buyer Protection Information
"You have heard eBay has a buyer protection program. Find out what it covers without using a search engine."

Task 4 - Save and Retrieve an Item
"You are not ready to buy yet. Save a product so you can come back to it later, then show me where you would find it."

Top Findings

#FindingDescriptionSeverityPriority
1 Homepage Clutter All 3 participants hesitated on the homepage and had trouble identifying where to start. Promotional banners and sponsored carousels obscured primary navigation. 3 High
2 Selling Flow Discovery All 3 participants struggled to find where to begin listing an item. Average time to locate the entry point was 4 minutes 12 seconds. 3 High
3 Bid vs. Buy Confusion 2 of 3 participants were unsure whether placing a bid had committed them to a purchase. One participant immediately searched for a way to cancel. 3 High
4 Seller Feedback Visibility 2 of 3 participants could not quickly find the seller's feedback score and did not use it to evaluate trust before bidding. 2 Medium
5 Filter Discoverability 2 of 3 participants never used the search filter panel and relied entirely on the default sort order throughout their sessions. 2 Medium
6 Checkout Error Messages 1 participant encountered a vague checkout error and spent over 3 minutes trying to resolve it before giving up on the task. 2 Medium

Six Specific Recommendations

  1. Add a prominent "Sell" button to the top navigation bar. The current "Sell" link is styled the same as other navigation items and is easy to miss. Making it a distinct, high-contrast button would help first-time sellers immediately identify the entry point without searching.
  2. Redesign the bid confirmation dialog to clearly distinguish a bid from a purchase. The confirmation screen should explicitly state "This is a bid, not a purchase," display the maximum bid amount, and use different visual styling than the Buy It Now flow to eliminate confusion.
  3. Reduce homepage content density. Limit the homepage to one promotional banner and no more than two recommendation carousels. Adding whitespace and making the primary navigation links larger would reduce cognitive load, especially for new users.
  4. Display the seller's feedback score and review count directly below the item title. Currently, seller trust information requires scrolling to find. Placing it above the fold would allow users to evaluate seller credibility without hunting for it.
  5. Make the search filter panel visible by default on results pages. The current sidebar is easy to overlook. Ensuring it is visible without scrolling, or adding a clearly labeled "Filter" button near the top of results, would improve discovery and efficiency.
  6. Rewrite checkout error messages to be specific and actionable. Generic messages like "Something went wrong" should be replaced with messages that identify the specific issue (e.g., "Billing address does not match your card") and include a direct link to where the user can fix it.

Conclusion

This usability evaluation of eBay.com identified problems at key user moments, especially for new sellers and users unfamiliar with auction mechanics. The three highest-severity findings - homepage clutter, selling flow discovery, and bid/purchase confusion - all contribute to a loss of user confidence and potential task abandonment. Implementing the recommendations above, particularly the first three, would lower the barrier to entry for first-time sellers and improve usability throughout the platform.
